# Strategic Targeting Analysis Methodology

## The "Perfect Storm" Framework

A high-value strategic account exhibits convergence across multiple dimensions that create urgency and budget for security investment. The analysis framework scores each dimension independently, then synthesizes them into an overall account assessment.

The strongest opportunities arise when **financial capacity + active threat pressure + regulatory deadline + accessible decision-maker** converge simultaneously.

---

## Scoring Dimensions

### 1. Financial Capacity

Determines the prospect's ability to invest in security services.

| Score | Criteria | Indicators |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **Unlimited** | Revenue > 10B | IT spend > 500M/yr, dedicated security budget, public security investments |
| **Substantial** | Revenue 1B - 10B | IT spend 100-500M/yr, CISO with direct board reporting |
| **Moderate** | Revenue 100M - 1B | IT spend 10-100M/yr, shared IT/security budget, ROI-focused decisions |
| **Budget-conscious** | Revenue < 100M | IT spend < 10M/yr, no dedicated security team, phased approach required |

**Key indicators beyond revenue:**
- IT Spend as % of revenue (industry benchmarks: Tech 8-12%, Financial 7-10%, Manufacturing 3-5%, Healthcare 4-7%)
- Security spend as % of IT budget (typical: 5-15%, post-breach: 15-25%)
- Recent funding rounds or IPO (growth = investment capacity)
- Public security hiring (signals active spend)

### 2. Threat Severity

Assesses the urgency of the prospect's security challenges.

| Score | Criteria | Indicators |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **CRITICAL** | Active, confirmed threats | Confirmed nation-state targeting, multiple breaches, insider incidents, 1000+ subdomains, internet-facing ICS/OT |
| **HIGH** | Significant threat exposure | Industry-targeted APTs, previous breach history, 500+ subdomains, cloud misconfigurations, weak email security |
| **MEDIUM** | Standard threat landscape | General cybercrime targeting, moderate attack surface, no confirmed incidents, basic security controls in place |

**Escalation factors** (increase severity by one level):
- Active APT campaign against the specific sector in the last 12 months
- Peer company in same sector recently breached
- Publicly disclosed vulnerability in their tech stack
- Executive leadership changes in security (new CISO = change agent)

### 3. Regulatory Pressure

Identifies compliance deadlines that create mandatory spend.

| Score | Criteria | Indicators |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **IMMEDIATE** | Deadline < 6 months | Active enforcement, penalties being issued to peers, audit scheduled |
| **HIGH** | Deadline 6-12 months | Regulation enacted, implementation guidance published, industry preparing |
| **MEDIUM** | Deadline 12-24 months | Regulation adopted, details being finalized, early movers preparing |
| **LOW** | No specific deadline | General best-practice compliance, voluntary frameworks |

**Key regulations to assess:**
- **NIS2** (EU) — Active since Jan 2025, essential/important entity classification
- **DORA** (EU Financial) — Active since Jan 2025, ICT risk management
- **CRA** (EU) — Reporting obligations Sept 2026, full compliance Dec 2027
- **GDPR** — Ongoing, enforcement increasing
- **Industry-specific:** HIPAA, PCI-DSS, SOC2, CMMC, etc.

### 4. Engagement Path

Evaluates the accessibility of decision-makers.

| Score | Criteria | Indicators |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **Direct** | Warm path to CISO/CIO | Mutual connections, previous relationship, shared events |
| **Networked** | Second-degree access | Team member knows someone at target, shared industry groups |
| **Cold** | No existing connections | Requires outbound campaign, event networking, or referral building |

**Priority contacts:**
1. CISO — Primary security decision maker
2. CIO/CIDO — IT budget authority
3. Security Directors/Managers — Technical influencers and champions
4. Risk/Compliance Officers — Regulatory urgency drivers

---

## Convergence Assessment

After scoring all dimensions, synthesize into the Perfect Storm table. The strongest accounts show:

- **Financial Capacity** of Substantial or Unlimited AND
- **Threat Severity** of HIGH or CRITICAL AND
- **Regulatory Pressure** of HIGH or IMMEDIATE AND
- **Engagement Path** of Direct or Networked

Accounts meeting all four criteria are STRATEGIC PARTNER candidates. Three of four = KEY ACCOUNT. Two of four = TARGET ACCOUNT.

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing the report, verify:

### Data Completeness
- [ ] Financial data gathered (revenue, profit, IT spend estimate)
- [ ] CISO/CIO identified with current titles
- [ ] Security team mapped (3+ contacts minimum for enterprise, 1+ for SMB)
- [ ] Attack surface data included (subdomains, cloud, email security)
- [ ] Threat actors mapped to target (sector + country match)
- [ ] Regulatory deadlines identified with specific dates
- [ ] Recent incidents documented (or confirmed no public incidents)

### Analysis Quality
- [ ] Financial capacity assessed with evidence (not just revenue)
- [ ] Pain points clearly linked to specific services
- [ ] Service values scaled appropriately to company size
- [ ] Engagement path is actionable (named contacts, specific connections)
- [ ] Competitive positioning is specific to this account (not generic)
- [ ] 3-year revenue opportunity calculated with year-by-year breakdown

### Report Quality
- [ ] Executive summary is compelling (money + pain + deadline convergence)
- [ ] All 6 parts completed with substantive content
- [ ] Tables formatted consistently throughout
- [ ] Action items have specific owners and deadlines
- [ ] Intelligence gaps clearly documented with impact assessment
- [ ] No placeholder text remains
- [ ] All claims supported by cited sources

---

## Key Success Factors

1. **Specificity over generality** — Named contacts, specific incidents, exact figures. "CISO John Smith (18 months in role, previously at Accenture)" beats "the CISO."
2. **Pain-to-service linkage** — Every service recommendation traces back to a discovered pain point. No orphan recommendations.
3. **Urgency drivers** — Clear reason to act NOW. Regulatory deadline, active threat campaign, or peer breach.
4. **Quantified opportunity** — Specific currency values, not wide ranges. Scale to company size.
5. **Actionable access path** — Clear route from current state to first meeting with decision maker.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Outdated contacts** — Always verify current CISO/CIO tenure via web search. Security leadership changes frequently.
2. **Generic threat intel** — Link specific threat actors to specific targeting of this company's sector and country. Don't just list top APTs.
3. **Vague financials** — Estimate IT spend from revenue using industry benchmarks. "Large company" is not a financial assessment.
4. **No urgency driver** — Every report needs a "why now" — regulation, incident, peer breach, or leadership change.
5. **Unrealistic pricing** — Scale service values to company size. A 50-person company doesn't need a 500K CTI program.
6. **Missing intelligence gaps** — Be honest about what you couldn't find. Gaps inform follow-up research priorities.
